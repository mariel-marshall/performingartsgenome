The Performing Arts Genome Project launches to power artificial intelligence in live arts

Dec. 5, 2019 (Toronto, Canada) ― A national coalition of industry and academic collaborators announced today the Performing Arts Genome Project: a comprehensive analysis of the performing arts to power personalized show recommendations.

Traditional approaches to recommendation systems, such as those for videos, music, or books, rely on analyzing the content and collecting user ratings and reviews over time to power the recommendations. The ephemeral nature of live arts requires a different approach: the domain needs to be carefully modeled so that shows can be recommended even before opening night.

The initiative puts into place the digital infrastructure for arts organizations across the country to grow their audiences using tools such as semantic discovery, personalized product recommendations and genre analysis for festival and season programming.

The Performing Arts Genome is built on a knowledge base of shows, companies and artists as well as reviews and ratings of artistic works. This data has been made possible by contributions from a number of industry stakeholders, including the Summerworks Performance Festival, Toronto Fringe, the Toronto Alliance for the Performing Arts (TAPA) and the Theatre Museum of Canada.

The Performing Arts Genome Project is comprised of two central parts a) conceiving and defining dimensions such as genres, mood, tone, form and structure, referred to as “genes” and b) applying these genes to companies, artists and works of live performance using a combination of natural language processing and manual curation - the “genome”. This work has a number of applications, including matching algorithms and semantic discovery tools that help people find and discover work that fits their taste profile.

“Everyone has a unique relationship with live performance – no one has exactly the same tastes. So delivering a great experience to every audience member requires a comprehensive and vast understanding of the entire sector, which simply doesn’t exist today,” says Mariel Marshall, CEO of Toronto-based startup StagePage Inc., an intelligence company that provides digital solutions for performing arts organizations to grow their audiences. StagePage is working in collaboration with leading knowledge engineer, Daniela Rosu, at the University of Toronto, to develop the knowledge framework and characteristics of the project.

Organizations with large-scale programming, including The Toronto Fringe, a grassroots, charitable organization which presents over 150 shows annually, are using the technology to power personalized show recommendations for their audiences.
